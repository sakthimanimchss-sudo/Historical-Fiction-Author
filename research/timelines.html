Historical Timelines

Journey through the eras that shape my stories

Home / Research / Timelines

History unfolds in layers—kings rise and fall, battles reshape nations, and ordinary people live out their lives against the backdrop of extraordinary events. These timelines trace the key moments that form the foundation of my novels, from the Viking Age to Roman Britain.

The Viking Age 793–1066 AD

The centuries when Norse warriors reshaped England

793 AD

Lindisfarne Raid

Vikings raid the monastery at Lindisfarne, marking the beginning of the Viking Age in England. The attack shocks Christian Europe and signals the start of centuries of Scandinavian raids and settlement.

First Viking Raid
865 AD

Great Heathen Army

A massive Viking force, led by the sons of Ragnar Lothbrok, lands in East Anglia. Unlike previous raiding parties, this army intends to conquer and settle—not just plunder.

Major Invasion
867 AD

Fall of York

The Vikings capture York (Jorvik), which becomes a major Scandinavian capital and trading center. Northumbria falls under Viking control.

Viking Capital
871 AD

Alfred becomes King

Alfred the Great ascends to the throne of Wessex at a time of crisis. He will spend the next decades fighting the Vikings and laying foundations for a unified England.

Rise of Alfred
878 AD

Battle of Edington

Alfred defeats Guthrum's Viking army, a turning point in the wars. The Treaty of Wedmore establishes the Danelaw, dividing England between Saxon and Viking rule.

Turning Point
886 AD

Alfred captures London

Alfred seizes London from the Vikings and begins refortifying it. He declares himself "King of the Anglo-Saxons," a step toward English unification.

Unification
991 AD

Battle of Maldon

A new wave of Viking raids begins. The Anglo-Saxon defeat at Maldon leads to the first payment of Danegeld—tribute paid to buy off Viking armies.

New Raids
1016 AD

Cnut becomes King

The Danish king Cnut the Great conquers England, becoming part of a North Sea empire that includes Denmark and Norway. Viking rule reaches its peak.

Danish Rule
1042 AD

Saxon Restoration

Edward the Confessor, son of Æthelred, returns from exile to claim the throne, restoring the Saxon royal line after decades of Danish rule.

Saxon Return
1066 AD

Battle of Stamford Bridge

Harold Godwinson defeats the last great Viking invasion under Harald Hardrada. Days later, Harold is killed at Hastings—the Viking Age ends, and Norman rule begins.

End of an Era

The Tudor Era 1485–1603 AD

The dynasty that transformed England

1485 AD

Battle of Bosworth Field

Henry Tudor defeats Richard III, ending the Wars of the Roses. He becomes Henry VII, founding the Tudor dynasty.

Tudor Begins
1509 AD

Henry VIII becomes King

The 17-year-old Henry VIII ascends the throne, marrying his brother's widow, Catherine of Aragon. His early reign is marked by war with France and cultural flowering.

Young Henry
1527 AD

The King's Great Matter

Henry seeks an annulment from Catherine, beginning the long process that will lead to England's break with Rome. Anne Boleyn becomes the focus of his desire.

Annulment Crisis
1533 AD

Anne Boleyn Crowned

Henry marries Anne, who is pregnant with the future Elizabeth I. Thomas Cranmer annuls Henry's first marriage, defying papal authority.

Anne's Rise
1534 AD

Act of Supremacy

Parliament declares Henry "Supreme Head of the Church of England." The English Reformation accelerates, dissolving monasteries and seizing church lands.

Break with Rome
1536 AD

Fall of Anne Boleyn

Anne is arrested, tried for treason, and executed. Within days, Henry marries Jane Seymour, who finally bears him a son, the future Edward VI.

Tragedy
1540 AD

Fall of Cromwell

Thomas Cromwell, architect of the Reformation, falls from favor and is executed. Henry marries Anne of Cleves briefly, then Catherine Howard.

Cromwell's End
1547 AD

Death of Henry VIII

The aging, obese king dies, leaving the throne to his nine-year-old son, Edward VI. The religious pendulum swings toward Protestantism.

Henry's End
1553-1558 AD

Bloody Mary

After Edward's early death, Mary I, daughter of Catherine, attempts to restore Catholicism, burning hundreds of Protestants. Her death brings Elizabeth to the throne.

Catholic Reaction
1558-1603 AD

The Elizabethan Age

Elizabeth I's long reign brings stability, cultural flourishing, and the defeat of the Spanish Armada. The Tudor dynasty ends childless with her death.

Golden Age

Roman Britain 43–410 AD

Four centuries of Roman rule on the island's edge

43 AD

Roman Invasion

Emperor Claudius orders the invasion of Britain. General Aulus Plautius leads four legions to victory against the native tribes. Britain becomes a Roman province.

Conquest Begins
60-61 AD

Boudica's Revolt

The Iceni queen Boudica leads a massive uprising, destroying three Roman cities. The revolt is brutally crushed, but Roman rule is permanently changed.

Major Rebellion
77-84 AD

Agricola's Campaigns

Governor Agricola extends Roman control into Wales and northern Britain, defeating the Caledonians at Mons Graupius. He begins consolidating Roman rule.

Northern Expansion
c. 108 AD

Mystery of the Ninth

The Ninth Legion Hispana vanishes from historical records around this time. Theories abound—destruction in battle, transfer to another province, or gradual disbandment.

Legendary Loss
122 AD

Hadrian's Wall Begins

Emperor Hadrian visits Britain and orders the construction of a wall across the narrowest point, marking the northern limit of Roman control.

Iconic Frontier
142 AD

Antonine Wall

Under Antoninus Pius, Romans push north and build a new wall between the Firths of Forth and Clyde. It's abandoned within two decades.

Short-lived Advance
197 AD

Division of Britain

Emperor Septimius Severus divides Britain into two provinces: Britannia Superior (south) and Britannia Inferior (north), improving administration.

Reorganization
c. 300 AD

Saxon Shore Forts

A chain of forts is built along the southeastern coast to defend against Saxon raiders. Roman Britain faces increasing external pressure.

Coastal Defense
383 AD

Troops Withdrawn

General Magnus Maximus takes troops from Britain to claim the imperial throne. Troops never fully return, leaving Britain increasingly vulnerable.

Beginning of End
410 AD

Roman Britain Ends

Emperor Honorius tells the British cities to look to their own defense. Roman administration effectively ends, though Romano-British culture persists.

End of Empire

These timelines represent key historical events that form the backdrop of my novels. For deeper dives into specific events or figures, explore the Real Figures and Primary Sources sections.
