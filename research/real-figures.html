Real Historical Figures

The people behind the stories

Home / Research / Real Figures

History is not just dates and events—it's the story of people who lived, loved, fought, and shaped the world around them. In my novels, I strive to bring these figures to life, honoring their true stories while imagining the moments history forgot. Here are some of the real people who walk through my pages.

Saxon & Viking Era

Alfred the Great

c. 849 – 899 AD

King of Wessex

The only English monarch to be called "the Great," Alfred defended his kingdom against Viking conquest and laid the foundations for a unified England. A scholar, warrior, and lawgiver, he personally translated Latin works into Old English and established a code of laws.

Appears in: The Saxon Chronicles

Guthrum

d. 890 AD

Viking King of East Anglia

One of the leaders of the Great Heathen Army, Guthrum fought against Alfred before being defeated at the Battle of Edington. He was baptized with Alfred as his godfather and ruled East Anglia as a Christian king, taking the baptismal name Athelstan.

Appears in: The Saxon Chronicles

Ubba Ragnarsson

d. 878 AD

Viking Chieftain

Son of the legendary Ragnar Lothbrok, Ubba was one of the commanders of the Great Heathen Army. Known for his ferocity in battle, he met his end at the Battle of Cynwit in Devon. His life and death are shrouded in legend and saga.

Appears in: The Saxon Chronicles

Tudor Era

Henry VIII

1491 – 1547

King of England

Perhaps England's most famous monarch, Henry VIII is remembered for his six wives and his break with the Roman Catholic Church. A Renaissance prince, he was athletic, educated, and charismatic in his youth, but grew increasingly tyrannical in his later years.

Appears in: The Tudor Intrigue

Anne Boleyn

c. 1501 – 1536

Queen of England

The second wife of Henry VIII, Anne was intelligent, ambitious, and controversial. Her failure to produce a male heir led to her downfall on charges of adultery, incest, and treason. She was executed at the Tower of London and is remembered as a key figure in the English Reformation.

Appears in: The Tudor Intrigue

Thomas Cromwell

c. 1485 – 1540

Chief Minister to Henry VIII

A brilliant and ruthless statesman, Cromwell rose from humble beginnings to become Henry's chief minister. He engineered the break with Rome, the dissolution of the monasteries, and Anne Boleyn's downfall—before falling from favor and being executed himself.

Appears in: The Tudor Intrigue

Thomas More

1478 – 1535

Lord Chancellor, Philosopher

A renowned scholar and author of "Utopia," More served as Henry VIII's Lord Chancellor. His refusal to accept the king's supremacy over the Church led to his execution for treason. He was canonized as a saint by the Catholic Church in 1935.

Appears in: The Tudor Intrigue

Catherine of Aragon

1485 – 1536

Queen of England

The first wife of Henry VIII, Catherine was a devout and determined queen. When Henry sought an annulment to marry Anne Boleyn, she fought vigorously for her marriage and her daughter Mary's rights, becoming a symbol of resistance.

Appears in: The Tudor Intrigue

Jane Seymour

c. 1508 – 1537

Queen of England

The third wife of Henry VIII, Jane was gentle and demure, often contrasted with Anne Boleyn. She finally gave Henry the male heir he desperately wanted, the future Edward VI, but died shortly after childbirth.

Appears in: The Tudor Intrigue

Roman Britain

Emperor Hadrian

76 – 138 AD

Roman Emperor

One of the "Five Good Emperors," Hadrian is best known for building Hadrian's Wall across northern Britain. A soldier-emperor, he spent much of his reign traveling the provinces, consolidating the empire's borders and promoting cultural and architectural projects.

Appears in: The Roman Eagle

Boudica

d. 60/61 AD

Queen of the Iceni

A warrior queen who led a major uprising against the Roman occupation. After her husband's death, the Romans seized her kingdom and humiliated her family. In response, she raised an army that destroyed three Roman cities before being defeated. Her fate remains unknown.

Appears in: The Roman Eagle

Gnaeus Julius Agricola

40 – 93 AD

Roman Governor of Britain

A successful general and governor who extended Roman control into what is now Scotland. His life was recorded by his son-in-law, the historian Tacitus. Agricola's campaigns and administrative reforms cemented Roman rule in Britain for centuries.

Appears in: The Roman Eagle
