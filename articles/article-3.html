The Art of Historical Dialogue

"Hark, good sir, I prithee, dost thou know the way to yonder tavern?" If I read that line in a historical novel, I would close the book. Good historical dialogue should transport readers to another time without making them feel like they need a translator. It's one of the most delicate balances a writer must strike.

In this article, I'll share the techniques I've developed over a decade of writing historical fiction—from the Viking Age to Tudor England to Roman Britain. Whether you're a fellow writer or simply curious about the craft, these insights will help you understand what makes characters from the past sound authentic without sacrificing readability.

The Authenticity Trap

The biggest mistake new historical fiction writers make is assuming that authentic dialogue means using archaic language. They fill their pages with "thee," "thou," "prithee," and "forsooth"—words that might appear in a Shakespeare play but would sound stilted and artificial in a novel.

The truth is, we don't actually know how people spoke in the past. We have written records, letters, and documents, but these are formal, edited texts—not transcripts of casual conversation. A letter from a Roman soldier on Hadrian's Wall might give us his vocabulary and grammar, but it can't tell us his tone of voice, his pacing, or his idioms.

"The goal of historical dialogue is not to reproduce how people actually spoke—that's impossible. The goal is to create the illusion of authenticity while maintaining clarity and emotional resonance for the modern reader."

— Hilary Mantel, from "The Guardian" interview

The solution is to aim for what I call "period-flavored modern English." Use a light touch with archaic terms, focus on sentence structure and rhythm, and let context do the heavy lifting.

Cadence and Rhythm

The most effective tool for creating historical feel isn't vocabulary—it's sentence structure. Different eras had different patterns of speech, influenced by the Bible, by formal education, by the rhythms of oral culture.

The Biblical Cadence

For medieval and early modern characters, I often draw on the rhythms of the King James Bible and the Book of Common Prayer. These texts shaped English speech for centuries. Listen to the parallelism, the balanced clauses, the formal but flowing structure:

Authentic Period Cadence:

"I have walked these roads for forty years, and never have I seen such a harvest. The Lord provides, my son, even when we doubt His grace."

Modern Equivalent:

"I've been walking these roads for forty years and I've never seen a harvest like this. God provides, even when we doubt him."

Roman Period Rhythm

For my Roman Eagle series, I studied Latin rhetoric and the speeches in Tacitus. Roman speech was more direct, more military in its precision, but still formal by modern standards. The key is shorter clauses and more imperative structures.

Roman Officer (c. 117 AD):

"The Ninth Legion did not simply vanish. Legions do not vanish. They are destroyed, or they desert, or they are recalled. Find me the truth, and find it quickly."

Choosing Vocabulary

Vocabulary requires the lightest touch. A few well-chosen period words can establish setting; too many will confuse and alienate readers.

The 5% Rule

I aim for no more than 5% of the dialogue to consist of period-specific vocabulary. That's one unfamiliar word per twenty familiar ones. The rest should be modern English—but modern English chosen for its timeless quality.

Words to Avoid

  • Modern slang and idioms: A Viking shouldn't say something "sucks" or is "awesome" in the modern sense.
  • Anachronistic concepts: No one in Roman Britain had a "medieval" mindset or talked about the "feudal system."
  • Overly technical jargon: Use period-appropriate terms for objects, but explain them through context.

Words to Use Sparingly

  • Titles and forms of address: "My lord," "sire," "dominus"—but vary them to avoid repetition.
  • Period exclamations: "By the gods!" "Christ's wounds!"—used once per chapter at most.
  • Archaic pronouns: "Thee," "thou," "thy" can work for specific characters (Quakers, the deeply religious), but not for everyone.

Class, Education, and Regional Speech

In any historical period, people spoke differently depending on their class, education, and region. A Roman legionary from Gaul would have different Latin than a senator from Rome. A Tudor noblewoman would speak differently than a merchant's wife.

Indicating Class Through Grammar

Rather than using dialect or misspellings (which can feel condescending), indicate class through sentence complexity and vocabulary range. Educated characters use more subordinate clauses, more varied vocabulary. Less educated characters speak in simpler, more direct sentences.

Roman Senator

"The province requires a governor of experience and judgment, not some boy whose only qualification is his family name. I have served in Britannia; I know its tribes, its weather, its legions."

Roman Legionary

"The new legate's a boy. Never fought north of the wall. The lads won't follow him."

Regional Flavor

Be extremely careful with regional dialect. A little goes a long way. A few word choices ("aye" for yes, "nowt" for nothing) can suggest Northern English speech without rendering the dialogue impenetrable.

Examples from Roman Britain

Let me show you how these principles work in practice, using examples from my Roman Eagle series.

Example 1: Officer to Soldier

Lucius (Centurion):

"You've served how many years, Decimus? Twelve? And still you jump at shadows. The tribes beyond the wall are no different from the Gauls my father fought—loud, brave, and undisciplined. Stand your ground."

Decimus (Legionary):

"The Gauls didn't have the dark, sir. They didn't have the mist. Out here, a man can't see what's coming."

Notice: No attempt at "authentic" Latin grammar. Instead, the military hierarchy comes through in the centurion's condescending tone and the soldier's informal "sir." The word choices are modern but the concerns are period.

Example 2: British Chieftain

Bran (Tribal Leader):

"The Romans build walls because they are afraid. They think stone will hold us back. But we have always been here. We will still be here when their walls crumble and their eagles are forgotten."

The poetry and rhythm suggest oral tradition. The vocabulary is simple but the structure is formal—appropriate for a leader addressing his people.

Example 3: Informal Conversation

Marcus:

"Did you see his face when the legate mentioned the Ninth?"

Gaius:

"Like he'd seen a ghost. Everyone knows they're gone. Why can't they just say it?"

Marcus:

"Because saying it makes it true. And truth is bad for morale."

This could be modern soldiers talking. That's intentional. The universality of human experience connects readers to the characters, while the context (the lost Ninth Legion) keeps it firmly historical.

Practical Exercises

Ready to practice? Here are three exercises to develop your historical dialogue skills:

Exercise 1: Translate Modern to Period

Take this modern conversation and rewrite it for a Viking Age setting, using the principles above:

"Hey, are you coming to the tavern tonight?" "Can't. I'm on guard duty." "That's too bad. We'll drink your share."

See Suggested Version

"Will you join us at the hall tonight?" "I cannot. The watch claims me." "A pity. We'll drink your portion."

Exercise 2: Class Distinction

Write the same basic conversation between a Tudor nobleman and his servant. Make their speech reflect their different stations.

See Suggested Version

Nobleman: "Has the post arrived from London?"
Servant: "Not yet, my lord. The roads are bad."
Nobleman: "The roads are always bad. Send a boy to the crossroads to watch."
Servant: "Right away, my lord."

Exercise 3: Period Flavor

Add period flavor to this neutral dialogue by adjusting vocabulary and rhythm, not by adding archaic words:

"I don't trust him. He's been asking too many questions." "What kind of questions?" "About the gold. Where it came from. Who else knows."

See Suggested Version

"I mislike his questions. Too many, and too pointed." "Asking what?" "The gold. Its source. Who shares the knowledge."

Remember: the goal is not to make your readers work to understand what's being said. It's to immerse them in another time while keeping the story moving. When you get it right, readers won't notice the dialogue at all—they'll simply hear the voices of the past.
