Decoding Tudor Handwriting: A Researcher's Guide

One of the greatest challenges facing any Tudor historian—whether academic or novelist—is the handwriting. The elegant but often baffling script of the 16th century, with its unfamiliar letterforms, rampant abbreviations, and inconsistent spelling, can feel like a foreign language. Yet learning to read these documents opens a direct window into the minds of people who lived five centuries ago.

In this guide, I'll share the techniques I've developed over years of working in archives, from the National Archives at Kew to the British Library. Whether you're a fellow writer, a student, or simply a history enthusiast, these skills will help you unlock the secrets of Tudor manuscripts.

Understanding Tudor Scripts

The 16th century saw a fascinating transition in English handwriting. Several different scripts were in use, often depending on the writer's education, social status, and the purpose of the document.

Secretary Hand

The most common script for everyday writing—letters, business records, legal documents—was secretary hand. Characterized by its angular forms, looped ascenders, and distinctive letter shapes, it can look almost like a different alphabet to modern eyes. Secretary hand evolved from medieval business scripts and was taught in grammar schools and writing masters' shops.

Example of secretary hand from a letter of Thomas Cromwell, c. 1535.

Italic Hand

Introduced from Italy during the Renaissance, italic hand was considered more elegant and modern. It was favored by scholars, aristocrats, and those with humanist educations. Queen Elizabeth I wrote a beautiful italic hand. Italic is much closer to modern handwriting and is generally easier for beginners to read.

Court Hand

Used in legal records and official documents, court hand was a specialized script full of abbreviations and unique forms. It persisted in legal contexts long after it had disappeared elsewhere, and remains one of the most challenging scripts for modern researchers.

Most documents mix these scripts. A letter might be written in secretary hand but have an italic signature. Legal documents often combine court hand for standard phrases with secretary for the main text.

Common Abbreviations and Contractions

Tudor writers were obsessed with saving space and time. Abbreviations are everywhere in manuscripts, and learning to recognize them is essential.

Common Abbreviation Marks

  • The macron (¯) over a letter: Indicates a missing 'm' or 'n'. "cōmon" = "common", "wˉ" = "with"
  • The 9-shaped symbol: Often represents "us" or "es". "hu9" = "huius" (Latin), "th9" = "thus"
  • The superscript line through 'p': "p̄" = "per", "par", or "pro" depending on context
  • The 'q' with a line: "q̄" = "que" (Latin for "and")
  • The ampersand (&): Used for "and" just as today, but often with elaborate flourishes

Common Word Abbreviations

wch which
yt that
ye the (from the Old English thorn character, pronounced "the")
yoͬ your
sd said
Lres letters
Ma tie majesty

"The key to reading abbreviations is context. The same symbol can mean different things in different words. Always read the whole sentence first, then go back to puzzle out the abbreviated words."

— Dr. Heather Wolfe, Folger Shakespeare Library

Deciphering Individual Letters

Secretary hand uses several letterforms that are very different from modern writing. Here are the most problematic letters to watch for:

Letter 'c'

Often looks like a modern 'r' or a backward 'c'. Sometimes written with a loop.

Letter 'e'

Usually a looped shape, often open at the top, sometimes looking like a figure 8.

Letter 'h'

The ascender often loops dramatically to the left, and the base can look like an 'n'.

Letter 'r'

Often looks like a modern 'v' or a 'u' with a hook. The 'r' in "your" is particularly tricky.

Letter 's'

Two forms: the long 's' (ſ) that looks like a modern 'f' without the crossbar, and the round 's' at the end of words.

Letter 'v' and 'u'

Often used interchangeably. "vpon" = "upon", "vnder" = "under".

Dates, Numbers, and Numerals

Tudor documents use several systems for writing numbers. Dates are particularly important—and often confusing.

Arabic vs. Roman Numerals

Both systems were in use. Arabic numerals (1,2,3) appear more frequently in commercial and everyday documents, while Roman numerals (I, II, III) were preferred for formal dates, regnal years, and legal documents.

Regnal Years

Legal documents often date by the year of the monarch's reign rather than the calendar year. "In the 28th year of the reign of our sovereign lord Henry VIII" means 1536-37 (depending on the exact date). Converting these requires a regnal year table.

The Double Dating Problem

Until 1752, the English new year began on March 25th (Lady Day). A document dated "February 1535" might actually be what we would now call February 1536. This is why you'll often see dates written as "1535/36" in modern scholarship.

Online Resources and Tools

You don't have to learn alone. Several excellent online resources can help you practice and reference:

  • The National Archives' Palaeography Tutorial: A free online course with graded exercises using real documents.
  • Folger Shakespeare Library's Early Modern Manuscripts Online: Thousands of transcribed documents with images.
  • British Library's Digitised Manuscripts: High-resolution images of major collections.
  • Scottish Handwriting.com: Excellent tutorials though focused on Scottish records.
  • Latin Palaeography Network: For documents in Latin, which many Tudor records still used.

Practice Exercises

The only way to improve is to practice regularly. Here are three exercises to start with:

Exercise 1: Compare and Contrast

Find a document that has both a printed transcription and an image. Cover the transcription and try to read a line at a time, then check your work. The Lisle Letters and State Papers are good sources for this.

Exercise 2: Transcribe a Letter

Choose a short, clear document—perhaps a single page of a letter. Work through it systematically, creating a line-by-line transcription. Don't worry about expanding abbreviations at first; just record what you see.

Exercise 3: Create an Alphabet Chart

From a document you're working on, create your own personal alphabet chart. Note how each letter is formed, and pay attention to variations—the same writer might form 'e' several different ways even on the same page.
